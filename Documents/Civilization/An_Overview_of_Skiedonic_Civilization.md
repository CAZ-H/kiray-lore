## The Greater Skiedonic Nation

The Greater Skiedonic Nation was a structured, cooperative civilization built around a central industrial hub that managed the majority of their governance, research, advanced manufacturing, and overall technological development. Unlike many societies that evolved through forceful colonization or violent conquest, the Skiedonic people preferred to be non-violent within their own affairs, and organized their civilization around mutual dependency and logistical efficiency, creating an expansive shipping network rather than engaging in resource wars. Their nonviolent predisposition shaped a collectivist culture that valued coordination, knowledge-sharing, and collective progress. Due to this structure however, it meant that the Nation was not a complete global superpower; there were some corners of the globe that had no connection to the Nation. At great distances from the central hub, resource availability grew more and more diluted, meaning there were fewer settlements attached to the shipping network. Nevertheless, there were individuals who traveled out into the wilderness to form their own small self-contained villages, and in a few rare instances, towns.

### Structure and Economy

At the heart of the Nation was a single, massive, industrial metropolis responsible for the majority of high-end industrial production, research, and technological advancement. Both heavy industrial equipment and common everyday goods were produced in vast amounts here, and then traded and shipped off to supply the more developed outskirts of the Nation. Many individuals worked in this city in various professions and trades, those closest to the middle working primarily in research, logistics, and other "soft" professions, and those nearer the outside of the core working primarily in industry and commerce. This core city was surrounded by a hierarchy of satellite settlements that were gradually consumed and rebuilt for more advanced purposes as the Nation grew:

**Secondary industrial and commercial cities:** Positioned near the core along waterways, coasts, or convenient routes of travel over land, these cities functioned as logistical hubs, processing raw materials from outlying regions and acting as intermediaries between the industrial heartland and the more rural territories. Trade and commerce was a priority in these cities, as they were responsible for shipping vast quantities of all kinds of goods; they were the lifeline for most smaller settlements. As the Nation grew, some of these cities were even in the process of becoming merged into the core city. Usually, these cities were responsible for producing electricity as well. Various electricity generation methods were used, but the use of combustion was not favored unless absolutely necessary.

**Resource-extraction towns:** Positioned near sources of essential materials, these towns specialized in mining and refining of resources, particularly metals such as copper, tin, and aluminum. Because iron was not abundant on Kiray, these metals were the foundation of Skiedonic metallurgy. These raw materials would be collected and used for trade with nearby industrial and commercial cities. These towns mostly produced refined materials for trade, but also could produce simple goods from those materials. They usually oversaw several smaller satellite villages which were responsible mostly for agriculture and other petty goods. The products of these villages would be pooled and redistributed as-needed to sustain them, and the excess traded with industrial and commercial cities.

**Agricultural villages:** The outermost layer of the civilization consisted of smaller settlements focused primarily on agriculture, animal husbandry, and self-sustaining craftsmanship. Many villages operated independently, producing goods with locally sourced materials, but they still relied on the greater shipping network for items they could not manufacture themselves, such as tools, everyday objects, and food during times of scarcity. Like all settlements in the Nation, these villages had access to clean water and electricity, but they lacked nonessential industrial equipment and very sophisticated consumer goods. These items had to be requested from the shipping network with justification, or specifically traded for by the consumer. Education in technical topics was not often available in these villages, but many individuals preferred life closer to nature. In particularly fertile areas near the core, these villages were not consumed by the Nation's growth, and remained as "bubbles" of agriculture surrounded by more advanced development.

**Outskirts:** Beyond the reach of the shipping network, there existed isolated settlements and independent villages, often formed by those who rejected the structured life of the Nation or were forced out due to resource limitations. These villages were entirely self-sufficient, producing everything they needed by hand from the materials available in their immediate environment. Some relied on barter systems, while others developed small, insular economies centered around unique local crafts or rare resources. Over time, these villages developed distinct cultural traditions, dialects, and survival techniques, diverging from the broader Skiedonic way of life. These outskirt settlements existed for a long enough period of time in Skiedonic history for individuals to even develop their own subtle distinct physical differences. These regions were often viewed as lawless or untamed by those in the core, yet they housed some of the most resilient and resourceful members of Skiedonic society.

This structured model of society allowed for efficiency and stability. Instead of conflicts over materials, Skiedonic civilization functioned via well-maintained trade routes along waterways, as well as high speed tube transport over land. Transportation was very efficient along the network, taking at most a day to cross the majority of the globe. Communication was nearly instantaneous, via a global network of interconnected devices. Most resource requisitions only took 2–4 days from processing to delivery all across the planet, and most regular operations were managed automatically by computers.

Where matters of trade were concerned, Skiedons used a renewable currency which was provided by their settlement in an equitable manner according to the settlement's production efficiency. Every individual received a basic amount derived from their settlement's earnings according to their occupation, age, and need, and could barter for more in exchange for goods and services with other individuals or with various departments of linked settlements.

However, the concentration of knowledge and industrial power within the core city led to a stark division in education and technological capability. Those in the metropolis lived highly sophisticated lives, often having never participated in manual labor, agriculture, or hunting because their way of life was provided for by layers of satellite cities. Their focus was instead primarily on various sciences, industry, infrastructure, logistics, and space exploration, with little to no emphasis on weaponry and defense. As a result, despite the success and sprawl of their civilization, they were ill-prepared for existential threats.

Those who lived in rural or industrial areas had a greater degree of self-sufficiency, but they were still supported by the overall infrastructure of the Nation. However, the further one traveled from the industrial core, the more primitive life became. But in the end, those furthest from the core were the ones to survive the Zorn's Ultimatum and their retribution.

### The Great Exodus

When the Zorn delivered the Ultimatum, demanding 80% of the population to be exported off-world for enslavement, the collective leadership of the Greater Skiedonic Nation recognized that compliance or resistance would both lead to annihilation. Their only choice was to flee. Given the existing societal structure, it was those closest to the industrial core; scientists, engineers, and the educated elite, who had access to the means of escape. The six World Ships, designed for deep-space habitation, were launched with as many of the most capable citizens as possible, leaving behind over 60% of the population, primarily those from the outer settlements, agricultural regions, resource-extraction towns, and the otherwise lower class.

The decision to abandon those left behind was not merely a matter of class hierarchy, but of pragmatism. The World Ships had very limited capacity, and only those with the necessary skills to maintain those ships, support the population within them, and to teach future generations to do the same, were prioritized. Those who remained behind faced the full wrath of the Zorn, enduring slavery or destruction, and the swift collapse of their civilization into the scattered, primitive remnants that exist now. Due to the highly localized nature of the core of their civilization, many outskirt settlements were able to take shelter before the Zorn attempted to raze all traces of Skiedonic life on Kiray.

### Legacy

The structured nature of the Greater Skiedonic Nation ultimately became its downfall. Its leaders and most influential upper-class citizens, far removed from hardship and ill-prepared for a global defense situation, immediately fled at the overwhelming danger the Zorn posed. The civilization's centralized structure meant that once the core and its close constituents were removed, the vast remaining population could not hope to stand against the Zorn when they returned.

Thousands of years later, the legacy of the Greater Skiedonic Nation exists only in the overgrown ruins on Kiray's surface and in the scattered descendants of those who took to the stars.

## Metallurgy on Kiray

The majority of advanced Skiedonic civilization was upheld by a metalworking industry primarily focused on copper-based alloys, particularly bronze, and aluminum bronze.

Early in its formation, Kiray was impacted by multiple asteroids very high in copper, tin, and aluminum content. This strongly biased evolution towards copper-based blood, but also meant that Skiedonic civilization did not grow to use iron and steel as its common metal of choice. Iron does exist on Kiray, but it is not plentiful enough or accessible enough to be used for everyday applications. By the end of the Skiedonic civilization, iron and steel was used almost exclusively for industrial manufacturing and high-performance applications. Other rare metals were also available such as gold, titanium, and tungsten, but were used for similar constrained purposes.

### Copper and Tin-Based Alloys

The first metal to be worked by Skiedons was copper, particularly native copper, which is metallic copper that naturally occurs in a pure state, making it a relatively simple material to manipulate. This ease of use gave rise to the first tools, marking the beginning of Skiedonic civilization. Over time, Skiedons refined their metalworking techniques, expanding their knowledge of metallurgy and ore extraction.

With tin being readily available in the planet's crust, the Skiedons soon discovered the process of alloying copper with tin to create bronze, one of the most important technological advances in their history. Bronze, with a hardness rating similar to that of iron, became a valuable material for tools, furniture, and architecture. It offered several advantages over pure copper, including increased durability and strength, making it ideal for everyday use by the individual. This early innovation, however, did not stop at simple bronze; the Skiedons' understanding of metallurgy grew deeper as they experimented with more complex alloys.

### The Discovery of Aluminum

Significant technological progress came when Skiedons developed the understanding and chemical processes required to extract aluminum. The planet's unique mineral composition allowed for a relatively high abundance of aluminum-bearing ores, but the metal itself is notoriously difficult to isolate. However, once they mastered the technique, aluminum became highly valued for applications where lightweight strength was paramount.

This discovery led to the creation of aluminum bronze, a fusion of aluminum with copper, that revolutionized Skiedonic technology. Aluminum bronze, with a hardness comparable to medium-carbon steel, was prized for its remarkable durability, corrosion resistance, and ability to withstand the rigors of advanced applications. The ability to forge aluminum into an alloy that could enhance the properties of copper allowed Skiedons to extend their supply of aluminum, a metal that was otherwise complex to manufacture, and break into other forms of industry that required materials with high wear-resistance.

### Rare Metals

By the end of Skiedonic civilization, iron was mostly reserved for niche applications, alongside other rare metals such as gold, titanium, and tungsten. These metals, although available in limited quantities, were prized for highly specialized uses, such as in the construction of advanced machinery, aerospace components, and high-performance tools. The scarcity of these metals meant that Skiedonic society had to be resourceful, recycling materials often, and creating alloys with properties optimized for particular needs.
