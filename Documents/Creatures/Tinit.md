# Tinit

Tinit is a common name for a family of horizontal bipedal scavengers, as well as the name for the most common species of this family. These creatures are notable for their adaptable diet and widespread distribution.

---

# Taxonomy

While the Tinit is the most common member of a family of horizontally oriented bipedal creatures, it is also the family term for an extended collection of alike creatures. This family has also been referred to as "T-nits" or "Nits".

This family contains various specializations such as the Tonit and the Tenit which are directly related to and are differentiated from Tinits by minor changes in their morphology and behavior. These creatures are known as "true Tinits", as they are often capable of cross-breeding with limited success.

This family also contains creatures that have significantly diverged from the true Tinit line and are entirely different species. These creatures may have similar body-plans, but have significant differences in morphology and behavior, such as the Vorkono.

---

# Morphology

A Tinit is a horizontally oriented bipedal creature with a short tail and a forwards-pointed head.

True Tinits generally have one large eye on top of the head, two whiskers on the underside of the head, a vertically-opening 2-part beak that can be shaped differently depending on diet, a short pointed tail, and a thick chitinous shell protecting their backs. They are Kirian ungulates; wherein they have small discrete toes and a pad of tough connective tissue on each foot.

The typical Tinit has sky-blue leathery skin, and a red chitinous shell. Certain mutations can swap one or both of these colors. The shell covers only its back and extends to wrap around and under the tail. The beak is large and broad compared to the head, and serves as a multi-tool for both scavenging and digging with its triangular shape and edge with tiny serrations, which is useful for cutting through debris underground, or sawing through carrion.

# Behavior

Tinits are highly social creatures, exhibiting complex group dynamics and co-operative behaviors, which is a key factor in their ability to thrive in wildly diverse environments.

## Social Structure

Tinits live in colonies, typically consisting of 10–30 individuals, though larger groups are not uncommon in resource-rich areas. Occasionally, colonies can be found to contain members of other Tinit species such as Tonits, but this is not preferred.

Tinits will dig burrows to house the colony. These burrows are usually very simple with one chamber for sleeping together as a group, and sometimes a second chamber for caring for eggs and offspring, both of which are packed with soft nesting materials.

These colonies lack structure or leadership, and are instead fluid, constantly changing as opportunity arises. There is no fixed social ranking within the colony, but larger or more assertive individuals may take priority for feeding.

Tinit colonies are tight-knit, and only accept outsiders when there are plenty of resources. Tinits can occasionally be found fighting "turf-wars" for the right to scavenge in an area, or to take scavenging areas from another colony. When a colony becomes too large, it's common for a prominent colony member to initiate a split, and leave with several other members.

## Communication

Tinits rely primarily on vocalization and pheromones to coordinate.

They can produce various sounds, including chirps, squawks, and clicks. These calls are used to signal food, announce danger, or to intimidate. They also use scent markers to guide group members to food sources or to the colony burrow.

## Reproductive Behavior

Tinits engage in simple social displays to show off their strength and good health. These displays often include tightly coordinated movements and trilling vocalizations.

As with most Kirian creatures, when Tinit eggs are laid, they are initially unfertilized. After an egg is laid, there is a short period where the egg casing is soft and permeable, where a mate can fertilize the egg using a gland located on the bridge of the beak. Some Tinits are capable of laying eggs, whereas others are capable of fertilizing them. Sometimes, a Tinit is capable of both, or neither.

A single Tinit will lay 2–4 eggs in a clutch, and young are fully grown after half of a Kirian year. Tinits will reproduce at most twice per year.

Tinits practice communal nesting, where eggs are laid in a shared nesting burrow, and colony members collectively guard and maintain them. Juvenile Tinits are also cared for by multiple adults with no single parent responsible. This communal approach ensures that young Tinits are always cared for.

## Foraging and Feeding Habits

Tinits are opportunistic scavengers with a highly adaptable diet. They consume a wide variety of organic matter, including insects and other small invertebrates; fresh meat from opportunistic finds; carrion at varying stages of decay; fruit, particularly those that are overripe or decaying; and detritus, including shed skin, exoskeletons, and shells, egg casings, and other byproducts of larger creatures.

They are typically cathemeral, active at various times throughout both the day and the night. A Tinit colony always has some members active while the others rest, following a staggered pattern. This reduces their risk of predation, and increases their foraging and scavenging efficacy.

Tinits scavenge together in loose groups, covering a wide area and communicating with vocalizations when food is discovered, often following the trails of predators or the smell of decay to locate food. When food is discovered, there is often a scramble to feed. Once they are sated, members of the colony will collect as much food as they can carry, and return to the colony where the excess is stored in caches nearby, by burying it or hiding it in crevices. Food is not stored in the colony burrow to prevent attracting predators. This behavior also helps them survive when food becomes scarce.

Tinits will also use their serrated beak to dig into the ground, searching for buried insects, larvae, or decomposing organic matter, though most of the time this occurs during times of scarcity, or while digging the colony burrow. Tinits prefer to scavenge on the surface due to there typically being better foraging and scavenging yield.

# Lifespan

The average lifespan for a Tinit is 5–8 Kirian years, however some have lived as long as 12. Most Tinits die of predation, rather than age-related causes.
